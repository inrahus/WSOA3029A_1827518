Reflection on Data-Driven Visualisations

Throughout the semester, I found myself struggling to express the ties between the technical and theoretical compents that would further my creative aspect. Although I personally feel that my visualisations were a result of my efforts to tackle the technical and porduce something or the other - in this reflective piece, I want to elaborate more on what I wanted to achieve with my visuals and how my perspective on artistic visuals has changed, as well as what I feel could have been better developed, and hwo I feel this impacted the result of my final visualisation.

With the task of displaying and conveying data visualisations about COVID, I was very interested and extemly excited to to venture into the artistic visualisation side of coding. My main goal, was to approahc the tasks from a visual perspective. I wanted my visualisations to look good To appeal. To resonate with a sense of artistic beauty/aesthetic. With the development of the semester, and with mostly recent encounters with theory, my perspective had definitely shifted. While engaging with the work of Viégas and Wattenberg, my definition and understanding of what artistic visulisations are, changed, and in that, I came to appreciate the fruits of my labour and the results of my efforts more clearly. Using the definition for artistic visualisation as being, based and backed on appropriate sources of data, while also engaging heavily with my intent in the creation (Viégas & Wattenberg, 2007) of my graphs (detailed individually beneath each graph), I do think I achieved my goal, but not in the sense that I was expecting nor by the standards I previously measured my work and quality of work by.

With my resulting works, I don't think that the mentality with which I approached the tasks allowed me to really consider elements of representation and storytellling as holistically as I should have. This is a clear aspect that could have been better considered and developed in my work. My focus on completing the visualisations, as a rpimary goal, left no room for me to really pursue a more accessible and target-audience oriented means of communication (Riche et al., 2018). I feel that if I had established this and clearly defined a story or target market, I could have more cohesively incorporated my visualisation sinto my site, while also creating a more tailored visual experience (Riche et al., 2018), rather than just a final, aesthetic visual with no real narrative or feeling.

What I feel I did to compensate for this lack of narrrative drive, was to include some aspects of it - especially inmy pursuit to integrate the visuals, interactivity and progression of my visuals with the site - particularly in my last two graph creations. I really aimed to use some sort of unification from the site - directly with the use of color, and in the final visulisation that I have now submitted, I attemted to create a sense of emergency with the colour gradient. Even though this part of my owrk was not whole-y complete and somewhat lacking, I do think that my level of skill and competency with JS - although improved - played a large role. Retropectively, I also have to admit that, even if I had defined aspects such as a target audience or general narrative pursuit - the focus on trying to get some sort of work out, to present a visual at the end of the day, could have possibly still resulted in a similar end as where I now find my work. Or maybe not.

In my final visulaisation, I wanted to challenge myself and my skills, and aim to represent something more than just data as it stands. I wanted to showcase my reformed idea of what an artistic visual was, and I wanted to create an interactivity that would display the contributing data that fueled the final graph. These were my new goals. And I do believe I achieved them.

In my graph, I created a level movement so that the viewer of the visualisation could click on each individual set of data, and see what components make up the data, while also showing the comparisons between the data. In the context of the data we werw working with for this project, I think this piece worked out extremely well within the bounds of it's accomplishment. The visual dataset was the same as the previous pie chart, but with this bar graph repreentation, it was much easier to showcase the rise in the numbers and the severity of the pandemic and it's effects, without denouncing the importance of the underlying data - which are the types of cases present. Although I wanted to show how severe the pandemic and COVID was, I also wanted to show the what the numbers really meant - what they were made of, what they actually represented - and with the leveling of the graphs, this was made possible. This was, instead of just fearing the rise in numbers, the viewer can see what the number actually is - and in recent months, they can see how the rise in reported total cases is actually significantly made up of recovered cases.

As a whole, this graph was to show two sides fo a coin. To show the viewer that COVID has a massive effect, but also that in that, there is a reassurance of recovery and decrease in acive cases and low death numbers. I feel this intent and the resulting display of data truely qualifies the work as an artistic visual by means of the definition I now prioritise and subverts the very narrow minded and aesthetic focused ideals I initally approached my visualisations with. Even though, with clearer intent, there could have been significant inprovements in the communcation and creative aspects of my visualisations, I do think that the effect of the theroetical shifted my critical perspective of my work, to a very inclusive and more direct approach to my work, and that it is clear in the progression of my data visuals displayed on the site.

References

Riche, N. H., Hurter, C., Diakopoulos, N., & Carpendale, S. (Eds.). (2018). Data-driven storytelling. CRC Press.

Viégas, F. B. and Wattenberg, M. (2007) ‘Artistic Data Visualization: Beyond Visual Analytics’, in Schuler, D. (ed.) Online Communities and Social Computing. Berlin, Heidelberg: Springer Berlin Heidelberg (Lecture Notes in Computer Science), pp. 182–191. doi: 10.1007/978-3-540-73257-0_21.
