The Ethical Contribution of Art to Technology

With the scientific nature of technology, there is a presented risk and extreme concern relayed in regards to the moral complexities of the subject, especially in the public eye. In this article, Roeser showcases how the use of art can positively impact the perpectives of risky technology through emotional-moral reflection produced by the arts engagement with the audience, while also creating a better environment for decisions around the risks presented by technological innovation, and therby allowing for a more morally balanced approach to the subject.

Roser introduced the topic of techno-art - art used to reflect and engage with the presented risky technologies available. Throughout history and even in the comtemporary spaces of today, we see this techno-art that encompasses the ideals of art alongside the use of current and developing technologies. Several modernist movements in the beginning of the 1900s actively embraced the efficiency and celebrated the contributions of technology and we see its presence still in the aspects of Contructivism and Futurism, and even in terms of literature in the popularity of science-fiction. Howveer, this is not always the case and we see how critical persectives on technology and risks presented by them have manifested in the existence of the dystopian genre accross mediums, as well as in the era and through the current romanticism of the Romanticism era.

In current developments, particularly on the visual art front, we see how the inclusion and interest in technology, and it's advancement, has lead artists to experiment and create new forms of artworks through the use of new materials and mediums. Artists like Stelarc, whose interests in bio-art, performance and robotics lead to an interesting implant surgery, are finding new ways to emotionally and provactively engage with their audiences and explore the uses and capabilites of technology, outside the linear functions they were designed for - allowing for a more conceptual branching of the developments - as well as their ethical and legal bounds.

Roeser continues and mentions how art like Stelarc's reaches the audience - the public - in a very direct manner to their emotional and imaginative extents. Roeser makes it explicit that the topic of techno-art's affect on the emotional, is lacking of academic backing, and this is a major aspect that needs to be addressed moving forward. The tangible effect of techno-art on the audience and their emotional responses to it, creates a space in which we can critically reflect on the technology presented, while also discussing and addressing the moral implications of such advancement. As mentioned in the article, if we take Stelarc and his ear-on-arm implant - we could see how the potential interest and fascination of technology and the extent of it's capabilites would increase - but we will also be exposed to and see the critical aspect of the abnormal phsycial production of a ear on an arm or the perspective that the art is a "waste" of the technology. With the furthering of investigations in to these responses to techno-art, the genre can serve as a prosperous grounds for the ethical basis of technology, it's uses and the ethical facets that need to be addressed surrounding it and from there, the public's responses to the technology in question.

A direct method of this is through the intergration of paticipatory technology assessment - which already exists to ensure democratic decisiveness on risky technology - and the genre of technoart. By allowing for artists to gauge and reflect on the permeation of the technology with the audience and their lives, we can address and target concerns about the risks of the technology, while also challenging and monitoring the legal and moral standpoint of the technology in a space that caters for the freedom of expression. However this raises concerns and challenges in the art community - as the merits on the artistic front are ambiguous, and the ethical implications - even in a space of expression - may seem chaotic and endangering. Although there is a lack of guidelines in this particular avenue of art, techno-art shoould operate on a baiss that is appropriately contained, constrained and cautioned.

As Roeser explains, this intergration and display in the real world context, could potentially allow us to overcome the Collingridge dilemma, and prevent the spread of unknown detriment presented by past technologies and the inability of society to timely regulate the effect of the technology. The introdution of techno-art in both the scientific and artistic fields brings forward a great chance to prevent the undesirable - the irreversible and unfortuante event that could take place. The ability of artists in the field to move past the limits of the developers and the scientific communities, allows us to more realistically and meaningfully explore the technology's risks and its permeation with society, as well as how the emotional response to such risky technology can allow for a reflection that morally guides the creation and development of potential and newer technologies.
