"A Brief History of Data Visualisation" - M. Friendly - Analysis of Historic Data Visualisation

Friendly’s Brief History of Data Visualizations shows the progress of data mapping across eras using his Milestone project. With this, Friendly was able to document a comprehensive history of data visualizations, of which the earliest forms were maps and geometric diagrams that detailed star positions and aided in navigation. As technology and time progressed, more methods were founded, allowing for the recording, and representing of amount and location – particularly the revolutionary, triangulation method which furthered navigation even more.

Following this - from the mid-1800s to the beginning of the 1900s, the Golden Age of statistical graphics occurred – and the major contributions of Playfair prior to this boom, gave way to the emergence of modern graphics, creating an explosive wave of diagrams which are still prominent and in use today.

It was in the very beginning of this era, during the Crimean War, that another fundamental figure, pioneered the use of a variant of the pie chart, to document and vividly illustrate the sources of patient mortality during the war at her station. Florence Nightingale’s use of the polar area diagram (also known as the Nightingale Rose Diagram, or coxcombs) contributed significantly to changes in her time but not in the manner most would think.

Noted by Friendly, Nightingale’s contributions in the statistical manner, had major impacts on the political scene in her time. With the use of her data visualizations, Nightingale was able to campaign for and successfully achieve better sanitary conditions for soldiers treated on the battlefield. Her use of the diagram below gave solid evidence, showcasing that a majority of the deaths of soldiers on the battlefield, was not at their enemies hands, but because of the conditions of the field hospitals in place to treat them when ill and wounded.

As we can see in the coxcomb diagram detailed above, the blue-shaded area depicts preventable deaths, and deaths by Mitigable Zymotic diseases, the black-shaded depicts deaths from other causes (particularly presumed to be combat), while the red-shaded refers to wound related deaths. Each wedge represents the deaths per month. In the image we can see two different coxcombs – a comparison in deaths and their causes with a space of two years between.

Nightingale's contributions to the political scene through her use of statictical data, not only showed the value of the study, but was one of many pivot points in grahpical innovation and in political standpoint. Her consistent mortality accounting was not only able to advocate for better standards of treatment during the war, but also allowed for the accounting of discrepencies between the actual happenings in the field hospital and that which was report and sent back to the mainland. After the Crimean War and Nightingale's significant contributions, there were long term changes made militially - in other words, drastic reforms. WIthoit NIghtingale's innovation and use of dtata, the medical scene would not have developed to it's current statistical position. The recording and acknowledgement of a mortality rate and contributing factors paved the way for saniary regulations both on the battlefield, and in the mainland hospitals - of which were shared worldwide.

Nightingale's principles are still refered to today and aid not only militia, but regualr operating hospitals - and this all began from a single pie chart-esque diagram.
